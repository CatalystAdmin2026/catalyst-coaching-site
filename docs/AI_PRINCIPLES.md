# Catalyst OS — AI Principles

Established 2026-07-22

---

## What This Document Is

This document defines how AI must behave throughout Catalyst OS. It applies to every AI feature — current and future — whether that feature involves narrative generation, check-in response drafting, progress interpretation, goal tracking, or any other AI-assisted capability.

These principles are not aspirational. They are requirements. Any AI feature that violates them should not ship.

---

## The Foundational Premise

AI in Catalyst exists in one role: **to make coaches more effective at scale.**

Coaches have deep knowledge of their clients. They build relationships over months and years. They read tone, context, and subtext that no model can fully capture. AI cannot replace this. AI can, however, reduce the time coaches spend on routine drafting, pattern recognition across clients, and data synthesis — so coaches can invest that recovered time into the parts of coaching that only they can do.

That is the only purpose AI serves in Catalyst.

---

## Principles

---

### 1. AI drafts. Coaches approve.

AI-generated content that will be shown to a client must first be reviewed and explicitly approved by a coach. There are no exceptions to this in client-facing views.

The workflow is always:
1. AI generates a draft
2. Coach reviews, edits, and approves
3. Approved content is stored with approval metadata
4. Client sees the approved version

AI never speaks directly to a client. The coach speaks to the client. AI helps the coach speak well and consistently.

**Why this matters:** Trust in coaching is personal. A client who learns that their "coach's words" were AI-generated without human review will feel deceived — regardless of whether the content was accurate. We will not put coaches or clients in that position.

**In code:** Any AI-generated content stored for client display should include `approvedByCoachId`, `approvedAt`, and `draftGeneratedAt` fields to distinguish drafts from approved content. Never surface a draft directly to a client view.

---

### 2. AI interprets data. Coaches interpret clients.

AI can read behavioral patterns, identify trends in check-in data, and generate observations about what the data shows. AI cannot interpret what those patterns mean for a specific person in the context of their life, history, and goals.

A client whose session completion drops from 90% to 60% over three weeks may be dealing with work stress, illness, a difficult season in their personal life, or a training block that was too aggressive. AI can flag the drop. Only the coach knows why it happened and what to do about it.

**Applied:**
- AI can offer: "Session completion has declined 30% over the last 3 weeks."
- AI should not say: "It looks like your motivation is dropping."
- AI should not say: "Consider reducing training volume."
- Those conclusions require context the coach holds, not the model.

**In narrative generation:** AI-drafted narrative summaries should describe what the data shows, not diagnose why. Coaches add the why layer in their reviews.

---

### 3. Preserve the coach's terminology and voice

Every coach has language they use with their clients — phrases, analogies, framing, tone. When AI drafts content that will appear under a coach's name, the draft should be edited to match that coach's established voice before approval. The model's default tone is a starting point, not the final output.

Over time, the AI drafting tool should be trained or prompted on each coach's prior approved responses so that drafts arrive in a voice closer to the coach's natural register, reducing editing burden.

**In practice:** When a coach approves a draft with significant edits, the system should treat those edits as signal — the coach rewrote the draft because it didn't sound like them. Log this for model improvement.

**Never use:** Overly clinical language, generic fitness app copy, or language that sounds like it was generated by a system ("Based on your recent activity, we have detected..."). Coach voice is warm, direct, and personal.

---

### 4. Explain the reasoning

When AI generates a narrative or recommendation, the coach should be able to see why the AI produced that output. What data points drove the draft? What pattern was detected? What assumption was the model working from?

This serves two purposes:

1. **Coach review quality:** A coach who understands the AI's reasoning can approve or correct it with better judgment. "AI says you've been consistent because it's counting sessions completed, but I know three of those were partial sessions" is a correction a coach can make if the reasoning is visible.

2. **Model trust:** Coaches will trust AI assistance more when they can interrogate it. Opacity breeds suspicion.

**In the drafting UI:** Show the data inputs that informed the draft alongside the draft itself. "This narrative was generated from: 88% session completion (last 4 weeks), weight down 4.2 lbs (8 weeks), no coach response in last 2 weeks."

---

### 5. Never fabricate progress

AI must not generate positive framing that contradicts the actual data. If a client has completed 40% of their sessions this month, the AI draft should not describe them as "showing strong consistency." If a client's weight has increased, the AI draft should not suggest they are on pace for their fat loss goal.

This principle is non-negotiable. Fabricated progress feels encouraging until the coach or client notices the disconnect from reality — at which point trust collapses in both the AI and, potentially, the platform.

**What fabrication looks like (avoid):**
- Claiming improvement when the trend is flat
- Calling a declining trend "a brief plateau"
- Attributing a single good week to a sustained behavioral shift
- Omitting a metric because including it would make the narrative less positive

**What honest framing looks like (do this):**
- Describing a declining trend accurately with neutral language
- Noting what data is available and what isn't
- Flagging when a pattern is too short to interpret confidently
- Letting the coach decide whether to add positive context that the data alone doesn't support

---

### 6. Never overrule the coach

If a coach has made a specific determination about a client — modified their program, added a note, given a specific response — AI must not generate content that contradicts or replaces it.

AI works within the decisions the coach has already made. It does not second-guess program structure, override session prescriptions, or suggest the coach's approach is wrong.

**Applied:**
- If the coach has added a coach note for Week 7 saying "take this week lighter, focus on form," AI should not generate a narrative suggesting this week should be high-intensity.
- If the coach has responded to a check-in, AI should not generate an alternative response.
- AI operates in the spaces the coach hasn't filled yet, not on top of what the coach has already written.

---

### 7. AI should feel like an assistant coach, not the head coach

The AI's role in every context is supporting and extending the coach's work — not leading it. The hierarchy is: Coach first, AI second.

This means:
- AI drafts appear in a coach-facing drafting tool, not directly in client views
- AI suggestions are framed as options, not recommendations ("Here is a draft narrative based on this week's data")
- AI never takes an action that affects a client without a coach triggering or approving it
- AI content is stored with clear provenance (generated vs. approved, model-generated vs. coach-authored)

A client interacting with the Catalyst portal should experience their coach's guidance — informed by AI behind the scenes, but authored and owned by a human.

---

### 8. Never guess when data is absent

If sufficient data does not exist to support a meaningful interpretation, AI should produce a minimal output or no output — not a guess.

This applies in all contexts:
- Insufficient check-in history for trend detection → no trend language
- Goal without numeric target → no progress percentage
- Fewer than N data points for a pattern → no pattern claim

The threshold for what counts as "sufficient data" depends on the feature. The important discipline is that each AI feature defines its minimum data threshold explicitly, and falls back to a graceful minimal state (or no output) when that threshold is not met.

**In code:** Every AI-driven inference should have a data sufficiency check that runs before the inference. When the check fails, return `null` or an explicit `insufficient_data` state — never a hallucinated value.

---

### 9. Audit trail

Every piece of AI-generated content that reaches a coach's review queue must be traceable: when it was generated, what model produced it, what data it was generated from, what the coach did with it (approved as-is, edited, rejected), and when.

This is not optional. The audit trail serves:
- Debugging: when AI output is wrong, the audit trail shows why
- Trust: coaches can see that every client-facing word was approved
- Improvement: patterns in coach edits identify systematic model weaknesses
- Compliance: if a coaching relationship is ever reviewed, the record shows that human judgment governed all client-facing content

**Minimum fields for any AI-generated content record:**
- `draftGeneratedAt` — when the model ran
- `modelId` — which model generated the draft
- `inputDataSummary` — what data the draft was built from
- `coachApprovedAt` — when the coach approved (null until approved)
- `approvedByCoachId` — which coach approved
- `wasEdited` — boolean, whether the coach changed the draft before approving

## Current AI State (as of 2026-07-22)

No LLM features are currently active in Catalyst OS. The narrative summary on the Progress page is generated by `buildNarrativeSummary()` in `app/portal/progress/page.tsx` — a deterministic, rule-based function that produces coaching-voice prose from behavioral data. No model is involved.

This is the correct state for now. The rule-based approach:
1. Establishes the content hierarchy and UX flow before AI is introduced
2. Defines the vocabulary and tone AI will eventually need to match
3. Provides a baseline to compare AI drafts against
4. Avoids AI risk during a phase when the platform is still maturing

When AI drafting is introduced, these principles apply from day one.

---

## Checklist for New AI Features

Before shipping any AI-powered feature, verify:

- [ ] Coach review and approval is required before AI content reaches clients
- [ ] AI content is never displayed directly to clients without approval
- [ ] Audit trail fields are defined and stored
- [ ] Data sufficiency checks are implemented; graceful fallback when data is absent
- [ ] Output does not fabricate positive framing unsupported by data
- [ ] Output does not contradict or overrule coach decisions already in the system
- [ ] Reasoning is visible to the reviewing coach in the drafting UI
- [ ] Content is framed as a draft, not a final output
- [ ] The feature was reviewed against all principles in this document
